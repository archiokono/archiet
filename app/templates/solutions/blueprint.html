1. Executive Summary

{# Narrative textarea — larger for exec summary #}
{# ── Guidance block ── #}
About this section

What it is: A concise narrative — typically 300–600 words — that answers three questions in plain language: what problem does this solution solve, what approach was chosen, and what are the most consequential decisions made. It is the only section guaranteed to be read by every stakeholder, so it must stand alone without requiring any other section as context.

What to include: The business problem or opportunity driving the work (be specific — "reduce manual reconciliation effort from 3 FTE to 0.5 FTE", not "improve efficiency"); the solution approach chosen and why alternatives were ruled out; the two or three architectural decisions that shaped everything else (e.g. event-driven over synchronous, cloud-native over lift-and-shift); key stakeholders and the systems in scope; what is explicitly out of scope; and a headline timeline and cost range.

Who reads it and why: C-suite and programme sponsors use it to make funding and governance decisions. ARB members use it to judge alignment with enterprise standards before reading the detail. Future architects and engineers use it to understand intent when maintaining or extending the solution years later. If this section is vague, every downstream reader fills the gaps with their own assumptions.

What "good" looks like: Someone with no prior knowledge of this project can read the summary in two minutes and correctly answer: what does this solution do, which systems are affected, what is the biggest risk, and when does it go live. Specific system names, team names, and measurable outcomes appear in the text. Generic phrases like "improve agility" or "enable digital transformation" do not appear without a concrete metric attached.

{# ── Section guidance blocks ── #} {% if section_id == 'vision_motivation' %}
About this section

What it is: The motivation layer of the architecture — the WHY that makes this solution necessary. It captures external pressures, goals the organisation is committing to, constraints that cannot be violated, and assumptions the architecture depends on. All decisions in later sections should trace back here; if they don't, something was designed without a business reason.

What to include: Business drivers (regulatory change, competitive pressure, cost reduction mandate — name the specific driver and its origin); goals with measurable outcomes and named owners ("Reduce MTTR for Tier-1 incidents from 4 h to 30 min — owned by Head of Operations"); assessments of the current state (which capabilities are weak, which applications are at end-of-life); principles the design must respect (e.g. "no new on-premise servers", "GDPR-compliant by default"); and explicit assumptions (e.g. "assumes SAP upgrade will be complete before Phase 2").

Who reads it and why: Architects use it to validate that design decisions are grounded in real business need. Programme governance boards use it to confirm the investment is justified. Future teams use it to understand why constraints exist before trying to remove them. Without a well-documented motivation layer, solutions drift as individuals make local decisions that contradict the original intent.

What "good" looks like: At least three named drivers with a stated business impact each. At least five goals that are specific and measurable, not aspirational. At least two constraints that visibly shaped architectural choices in later sections. Every assumption is listed even if it seems obvious — unwritten assumptions are the root cause of most architecture failures.

{% elif section_id == 'value_stream_map' %}
About this section

What it is: The strategy layer — the bridge between why this solution exists (Goals, Drivers) and what the business does (Processes, Actors). It answers: which Capabilities must exist, in what sequence, to deliver the promised Outcome? Everything below — applications, data, infrastructure — exists to realise this chain.

Required ArchiMate chain — scored

  Goal  ←— realizes ——  ValueStream  —— uses ——  Capability
                  ¦                                   ¦
                  ¦         stages (triggering)        ¦
                  ¦  Stage1 → Stage2 → Stage3 — uses — Capability
  ValueStream  —— realization ——  Outcome
35 pts Goal → ValueStream (realization) — add a Goal in Vision & Motivation first, then draw a realization relationship from it to the ValueStream here. The stream is the mechanism by which the Goal becomes real.
35 pts ValueStream → Capability (association) — name the organisational capabilities each stage depends on. These are Strategy-layer Capability elements (“Real-time Monitoring”, “Automated Alerting”) — not applications. The Capability is then realized by BusinessProcesses below.
30 pts ValueStream → Outcome (realization) — the measurable result signalling the stream completed. “MTTR ≤ 30 min” is an Outcome; “improve MTTR” is a Goal. Distinct ArchiMate elements with different roles in the chain.

Stages: Each stage is a child ValueStream element linked in sequence via triggering (Stage 1 → Stage 2 → Stage 3). Each stage uses one or more Capabilities. A reviewer should be able to trace from any Goal in Section 2 through a stage to the Capability it requires and down to a BusinessProcess in Section 4 that delivers it.

What good looks like: Three to six named stages, each with an actor, a system, and the Capability it depends on. An Outcome with a measurable target (SLA, £ saved, error rate %). Individual elements without relationships score partial credit only — the chain score rewards connected architecture, not element lists.

{% include 'solutions/partials/_blueprint_capabilities.html' %} {% elif section_id == 'business_process_view' %}
About this section

What it is: A detailed view of the business processes this solution changes, supports, or replaces — at the level of individual tasks and decision points, not just high-level steps. Where the value stream map shows the journey, the business process view shows the road: every turn, junction, and possible detour. It is the primary input for change management, user training, and test case design.

What to include: Process flows with swim lanes organised by actor or department; decision points and the logic that determines which path is taken; exception paths (what happens when a step fails, times out, or produces an invalid result); manual approval gates and their escalation rules; the specific screens, forms, or APIs a user interacts with at each step; integration touchpoints where the process crosses into another system; and any steps that are being eliminated, automated, or significantly changed by this solution.

Who reads it and why: Business analysts use it to validate scope and identify missed requirements. Change managers use it to plan organisational impact and design training. QA engineers use it to derive test cases — every branch in the process is a test case waiting to be written. Operations teams use it to understand what breaks when a system is unavailable.

What "good" looks like: Every human-facing touchpoint has a named actor and a named system. Exception and error paths are documented with the same level of detail as the happy path — they are not marked "TBD." The process model has been reviewed by someone who performs the work today and confirmed as accurate. New steps introduced by this solution are clearly distinguished from existing steps that are being retained.

{% elif section_id == 'application_cooperation' %}
About this section

What it is: The application layer of the architecture — which software systems are involved, how they cooperate, and through what interfaces they exchange data. It answers the question "when system A needs to tell system B something, how does that actually happen?" This section drives API design, integration development, and determines how complex the solution is to build, test, and operate.

What to include: Every application component in scope (new and existing); the integration pattern for each system-to-system interaction (synchronous REST, asynchronous event, file transfer, database replication — be specific); sequence diagrams or interaction descriptions for the key flows; API contracts or message schemas for critical interfaces; error handling strategy for each integration (what happens on timeout, network failure, or invalid data); and data transformation rules where formats differ between systems.

Who reads it and why: Integration architects use it to design APIs and middleware. Development teams use it to scope their work and identify dependencies. Operations teams use it to plan monitoring — every integration point is a failure point that needs an alert. The more precisely this section is written, the fewer surprises appear during development.

What "good" looks like: Every system-to-system interaction is named, has a stated protocol, and has an error handling strategy. There are no "and then magic happens" gaps where data moves between systems without explanation. API contracts reference real endpoints or schema definitions. The section distinguishes between synchronous calls (where the caller waits) and asynchronous events (where it does not) — this distinction determines retry logic, latency budgets, and failure modes.

{% elif section_id == 'data_information' %}
About this section

What it is: The data architecture — what information exists in this solution, how it is structured, who owns it, and how it flows from creation to consumption to archival. Data decisions made here constrain every other layer: a poorly defined data model forces integration workarounds, creates reporting blind spots, and generates compliance exposure. This section is the most consequential to get right and the most expensive to fix once development has started.

What to include: Canonical data entities and their key attributes (name, type, owner, source of truth); data flows showing where each entity is created, transformed, and consumed; data quality rules and what happens when data does not meet them; data ownership by domain and the governance model for disputed data; retention, archival, and deletion policies with regulatory references where applicable; master data management approach (which system is the golden record for each entity, how conflicts are resolved); and any data migration required from legacy systems including transformation rules and acceptance criteria.

Who reads it and why: Data architects use it to design schemas and pipelines. Compliance and privacy officers use it to assess GDPR, data residency, and audit requirements. Integration developers use it to understand what data they will receive and in what format. Analysts use it to understand what reporting is possible. Poor data architecture is the single most common root cause of failed enterprise integrations.

What "good" looks like: Every significant data entity has a named owner, a single source of truth, and at least one stated quality rule. Data flows cover both the happy path and failure scenarios (what happens to a message if the destination is unavailable). Retention policies exist for every entity class, with a regulatory reference where relevant. The data model has been reviewed by someone who will actually query it in production.

{% elif section_id == 'deployment_view' %}
About this section

What it is: How the solution will be physically deployed and how it will transition from the current operating state to the future one. It covers infrastructure topology, environment strategy, the deployment pipeline, the migration approach, and what happens if something goes wrong. The migration plan in this section carries more project risk than any other architectural decision — a technically excellent design can still fail if the transition plan is unrealistic.

What to include: Infrastructure topology diagram or description (cloud provider and region, on-premise data centres, hybrid connectivity); environment strategy (dev, test, staging, production — and who can deploy to each); CI/CD pipeline approach (tools, gates, automated test requirements before promotion); migration approach (big bang vs phased vs parallel run — with justification for the choice); rollback strategy including the trigger condition ("if X happens within Y hours, we revert") and the rollback procedure; estimated downtime window and the business approval required for it; and a day-zero operations checklist (what monitoring is in place, who is on call, what alerts fire, who receives them).

Who reads it and why: Infrastructure and DevOps engineers use it to provision and configure environments. Release managers use it to plan go-live windows and communicate to the business. Operations teams use it to prepare support runbooks. Governance boards use it to assess deployment risk before approving go-live. The operations team that inherits this solution in two years uses it to understand how it was put together.

What "good" looks like: The migration plan names the rollback trigger, the rollback procedure, and the maximum tolerable downtime. The environment strategy is specific enough that a new engineer can set up a local environment from it. The day-zero checklist exists and has been rehearsed in a non-production environment. "Cloud deployment" without a named provider, region, service tier, and cost estimate is not good enough.

{% elif section_id == 'network_communication' %}
About this section

What it is: The technology layer — network topology, communication protocols, security zones, and the physical or virtual infrastructure that carries the solution's traffic. Network decisions affect performance, compliance, and cost in ways that are extremely expensive to change post-deployment. This section is where latency budgets are established, encryption requirements are confirmed, and firewall rule changes are catalogued before they surprise the security team at go-live.

What to include: Network zone diagram (DMZ, internal, restricted — and which components live in each); the protocol for every communication path in the application cooperation view (HTTPS, AMQP, gRPC, SFTP — with port numbers); latency and bandwidth requirements for each major data flow; load balancing and CDN strategy; DNS and certificate management approach (who owns the certs, how long they are valid, what the rotation process is); any new firewall rules required, stated as source/destination/port/protocol; and whether any traffic crosses organisational or geographic boundaries with the compliance implications.

Who reads it and why: Network engineers use it to provision routing and firewall rules before the solution can be tested end-to-end — these changes often have multi-week lead times. Security architects use it to assess attack surface and confirm encryption coverage. Compliance officers use it to verify data residency and cross-border transfer rules. These are the stakeholders most likely to block a go-live if their questions are not answered in writing.

What "good" looks like: Every communication path has a protocol, a port, and a classification (internal/external, encrypted/unencrypted). Every security zone crossing is explicitly documented with the firewall rule required. Latency budgets are stated as numbers, not as "should be fast." Certificate expiry dates are recorded and there is a named person responsible for rotation. A network engineer who has never seen this solution can derive their firewall change request directly from this section.

{% elif section_id == 'gap_analysis' %}
About this section

What it is: A structured comparison of current architecture versus target architecture — what capabilities, applications, data structures, or infrastructure elements are missing, insufficient, or need to change. The gap analysis is the business case written in architectural language: it quantifies why the current state cannot serve the goals in Section 2 and what specifically must change. Without it, the solution is a list of features with no explanation of why they were chosen over alternatives.

What to include: A current state inventory of the relevant capability or domain (applications, data, integrations, infrastructure that exist today); target state requirements derived from Sections 2 and 4; a gap for each layer — business process, application, data, technology — stating what is missing and what the business consequence of the gap is; a prioritised list of gaps (high/medium/low) with the business impact of each; reuse opportunities (what existing assets can serve the target state without change or with minor change); and explicit disposal decisions (what is being retired and why).

Who reads it and why: Programme managers use it to scope the transformation and estimate effort. Portfolio architects use it to identify reuse opportunities across other programmes. Investment committees use it to justify the spend — a gap that cannot be traced to a business driver should not exist. This section is also the primary source for Section 9 (Transition Roadmap) sequencing decisions.

What "good" looks like: Gaps are numbered and prioritised. Each gap has a stated business impact ("Without closing this gap, we cannot achieve Goal G3 — reduce MTTR to 30 min"). Each gap traces to a driver in Section 2 and contributes to a work package in Section 11. Reuse opportunities are quantified: "CRM module X satisfies 70% of the customer data requirement without modification." Gaps marked "low priority" still have a resolution path or an explicit acceptance decision.

{% elif section_id == 'transition_roadmap' %}
About this section

What it is: The sequenced path from current state to target state, broken into plateaus — stable interim states where the organisation can pause, consolidate, and realise benefits before moving to the next phase. A roadmap without plateaus is a waterfall; a roadmap without milestones is a wish list. The transition roadmap is the commitment the architecture team makes to the business, so it must be realistic, not optimistic.

What to include: Plateaus with named dates and a description of the stable state achieved at each one; the work packages completed in each plateau (reference Section 11); hard dependencies between phases — "Phase 2 cannot start until Phase 1's data migration has passed acceptance testing"; go/no-go decision gates with named decision makers and criteria; resource requirements per phase (teams, vendors, budget draw-down); benefits realisation schedule — which business benefits become available at each plateau and how they will be measured; and the critical path — which sequence of activities determines the overall timeline.

Who reads it and why: Programme directors and PMOs use it to staff projects and manage dependencies. Steering committees use it at governance gates to approve progression to the next phase. Vendor and partner teams use it to plan their own resource allocation. Business stakeholders use it to plan organisational change and benefits realisation activities. An unrealistic roadmap destroys trust; an overly conservative one wastes investment.

What "good" looks like: Each plateau has a named date, a named set of deliverables, and a measurable business benefit that can be confirmed without reading the rest of the document. Dependencies are explicit and directed — "Phase 2 requires X, which is delivered in Phase 1" — not implicit. The critical path is identified and the risks on that path are addressed in Section 12. The roadmap has been reviewed by the delivery leads who will execute it and confirmed as achievable.

{% elif section_id == 'work_packages' %}
About this section

What it is: The discrete, executable units of work that realise the transition roadmap. Each work package is a bounded scope of activity with an owner, a cost estimate, dependencies, and acceptance criteria. Work packages are the bridge between architecture and delivery: the architecture team defines them, the delivery team executes them. Vague work packages become missed milestones — the more precisely they are defined here, the fewer change requests appear during delivery.

What to include: For each work package: a short name and a scope statement ("Deploy API gateway and configure routing for 12 upstream consumers — excludes consumer application changes"); the responsible team or vendor; effort estimate in person-days or story points with a confidence level; cost estimate in currency with assumptions stated; dependencies on other work packages or external events; acceptance criteria written as observable outcomes a non-technical stakeholder can verify; and lessons learned from similar work in this organisation (what was underestimated, what surprised the team).

Who reads it and why: Project managers use it to create work breakdown structures and assign resources. Finance uses it to create budget lines and track actuals against estimates. Governance boards use it at stage gates to approve funding for the next phase. Delivery leads use it to negotiate scope when timelines compress. Lessons learned make future estimates more accurate and prevent teams from repeating known mistakes.

What "good" looks like: Every work package has a named owner, a currency estimate (even a rough order of magnitude), and at least one acceptance criterion a non-technical stakeholder can verify. The sum of all work package estimates is consistent with the overall programme budget. Dependencies are directed and complete — if WP-3 depends on WP-1, WP-1 knows about it. Acceptance criteria use the pattern "given X, when Y, then Z" — not "feature is working."

{% elif section_id == 'security_viewpoint' %}
About this section

What it is: The security architecture — threat model, control framework, risk posture, and compliance mapping for this solution. Security controls designed into the architecture at this stage cost a fraction of what they cost to retrofit after development. This section is not a checklist of security best practices; it is a structured argument that the solution's specific threat landscape has been analysed and the controls chosen are proportionate to the risks identified.

What to include: Threat actors and their likely attack vectors against this specific solution (not generic threats — name what is realistic given the data handled and the exposure surface); security controls by layer (network isolation, application authentication and authorisation, data encryption at rest and in transit, identity and access management, audit logging); regulatory and compliance requirements with specific articles or controls mapped to architectural decisions (e.g. "GDPR Art.32 — satisfied by AES-256 encryption of PII at rest in the data warehouse"); residual risks with a stated risk owner and acceptance decision; and penetration testing scope and schedule.

Who reads it and why: Security architects use it to validate that the design follows enterprise security standards. The CISO office uses it to maintain the organisational risk register. External auditors use it as evidence of due diligence during compliance assessments. Developers use it to understand what security requirements they are responsible for implementing. A security section that says "we will follow security best practices" provides no assurance to any of these readers.

What "good" looks like: Every identified threat has a stated control and a residual risk rating (accepted/mitigated/transferred). Compliance requirements are mapped to specific architectural decisions, not just listed. The threat model names realistic adversaries for this solution's context — not a generic list of OWASP categories. A penetration test scope has been agreed with the security team and a date is in the project plan. Security is treated as an architectural quality, not a project phase.

{% elif section_id == 'nfr_satisfaction' %}
About this section

What it is: Proof that the architecture meets the non-functional requirements — the quality attributes that determine whether the solution is usable and operable in production, not just functionally correct. NFRs that are not designed in from the architecture stage always become expensive incidents: the database that was not sized for peak load, the API that times out under real traffic, the service that cannot be restored in an acceptable RTO. This section is the architecture team's warranty on system quality.

What to include: For each NFR category (performance, availability, scalability, maintainability, recoverability, security — add others relevant to this solution): the target metric stated as a number with units ("99.9% uptime = max 8.76 hours downtime per year across all planned and unplanned outages"); the architectural mechanism or decision that achieves it ("Active-passive failover with 60-second automatic failover, data replicated synchronously to standby"); the current baseline if known; the test approach that will verify the target is met before go-live ("Load test with 2× expected peak concurrent users for 30 minutes, monitored by APM"); and the named owner responsible for that NFR in production.

Who reads it and why: Engineering leads use it to validate that the design can physically achieve the targets before committing to them. Operations and SRE teams use it to design monitoring thresholds and on-call runbooks. Service level managers use it to negotiate SLAs with the business. Capacity planners use it to size infrastructure. NFRs that are agreed verbally but not written here get forgotten in delivery and discovered in production.

What "good" looks like: Every NFR has a number, not an adjective. "Fast" is not an NFR; "P95 response time under 200ms at 500 concurrent users" is. Every NFR has an architectural decision that achieves it and a test that can verify it before go-live — if there is no test, there is no confidence. The NFR targets have been agreed with the business owner who will be affected if they are missed, not set unilaterally by the architecture team.

14. Requirements Traceability

{# ── Guidance block ── #}
About this section

What it is: A matrix that proves every stated requirement has been addressed by an architectural decision and can be verified by a test — and that nothing was forgotten. Traceability is the architecture team's accountability mechanism: it makes gaps visible rather than hidden, and it gives governance boards objective evidence that the design is complete before approving go-live. A solution with no traceability matrix has no systematic way to know what it has and has not addressed.

What to include: For each requirement: a unique ID and a short statement of the requirement; the source of the requirement (a stakeholder, a regulation, a driver from Section 2, or an NFR from Section 13); the architectural element or decision that satisfies it (with a reference to which section of this blueprint contains that decision); the test case or acceptance criterion that verifies it has been implemented correctly; and the status (traced and tested / traced but not yet tested / untested gap / explicitly deferred with justification). The matrix should be exhaustive — every requirement in scope, including the ones that are difficult to trace.

Who reads it and why: QA leads use it to ensure test coverage matches architectural intent — missing traces are missing tests. Governance boards use it at stage gates to confirm the design is complete before approving funding for the next phase. Compliance auditors use it to verify regulatory requirements have been addressed. Project sponsors use it to confirm that the original business drivers from Section 2 are actually satisfied by the architecture, not just mentioned in it. Traceability is the closing argument of the blueprint.

What "good" looks like: Coverage is 100% — every requirement in scope has an entry, even requirements that are explicitly out of scope or deferred (those need a row with a clear status and justification, not omission). Every traced requirement has both an architectural decision reference AND a test reference — a requirement that is designed for but not tested is not satisfied. Gaps are explicitly flagged in red, not silently omitted. The matrix has been reviewed by a QA lead and a business analyst, not just the architect who wrote the blueprint.
